Chillennium

Chillennium is an annual student-led 48 hour game design competition. A theme is provided by the organization and then teams of up to 4 work together to design a game within the allotted time and in accordance with the given theme. The games are graded by judges after the game jam is finished. Awards are then given out according to the grade relative to all other games. I have participated every year since 2016.

In Chillennium 2016, I was in a group with 3 other people I had never met before- two artists and an animator. I was programmer for this game. This was my first Chillennium and my first time using Unity and C#. The theme for this game jam was "foofaraw". We created 2D platformer with 3 levels where the main character was a princess that yielded a realistic-looking pistol that she used to defeat enemies in her way. Since I was the only programmer, I constructed the game physics and rules.

In Chillennium 2017, I was in a group with one friend and two strangers- the two stragers were artists, and my friend and I were programmers. I learned from Chillennium 2016 that an iterative level-based game was very hard to make in 48 hours, so I went in to this Chillennium with the idea to create a shorter game that can be replayed multiple times. The theme for this game jam was "role reversal". The game we created was a two-player 2D game where every 30 seconds, the controls for each player switches, causing them to control the other player's character. The goal of the game is to fill up a wheel barrel in the center of the level with a certain amount of candy. However, the two players are also competing to see who can contribute more candy to the pile. Whoever contributes more candy to the pile once the candy quota is met is the winner of the game. I worked on the game physics, rules, and animations.

In Chillennium 2018, I was in a group with one friend and one stranger- all of us were programmers, but one of us was assigned to be an artist. The theme for this game jam was "transformation". The game we created was a 2D puzzle-platformer. The player is meant to control a construction worker to reach the flag at the end of each level. However, there are not enough platforms for the player to reach the goal, so we implemented a building system where the player can place a limited number of platforms in order to reach the goal. I worked on the game physics, rules, the level design of each stage, and animations. This game was a Chillennium Award Finalist.

I participated in the Fall 2016 TAMUhack individually. I practiced my skills with Python and created a simple statistical analysis program that, when given a set of data, can return the mean, median, mode, range, standard deviation, and various other data.

I participated in the Spring 2019 TAMUhack in a group of 5 programmers. We worked on a browser application using Node.js. Our goal was to create an online multiplayer game where users are all presented wth a tweet from Twitter and are prompted to give the tweet a hashtag. All players vote on their favorite hashtag, and the user with the most votes at the end of the round is the winner. I worked on the front end of the website with 1 other person and we used HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

HowdyHack is a hackathon that is hosted by Aggie Coding Club, and the rules are the same as TAMUhack. I worked with one other person when I participated in the Fall 2018 HowdyHack. Together, we brainstormed an idea to create a chatbot that professors could use to provide answers to their students for frequently asked questions. For example, if a student wants to know the date of the next exam, rather than sending an email to the professor, the idea was to make a chatbot that a professor can customize and link on their teacher website that will answer the student's question.
